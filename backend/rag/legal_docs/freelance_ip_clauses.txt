FREELANCE CONTRACT CLAUSES — PAKISTANI FREELANCERS WITH INTERNATIONAL CLIENTS

CONTEXT
Pakistani freelancers frequently sign contracts drafted by foreign clients 
in the US, UK, EU, and elsewhere. These contracts are often written under 
foreign law and contain clauses that are aggressive by Pakistani standards 
or that quietly transfer significant rights. The following covers the most 
important clauses to analyse.

IP OWNERSHIP — WORK FOR HIRE
Most international freelance contracts contain a work for hire clause stating 
that all work created under the contract belongs to the client, not the 
freelancer. This is standard and expected. It is not a red flag unless it 
extends to work created outside the scope of the contract.

IP OWNERSHIP — BACKGROUND IP
Some contracts claim ownership of tools, frameworks, libraries, or processes 
the freelancer brings to the project. This is aggressive and a red flag. 
A freelancer's pre-existing code libraries, processes, and methods should 
remain the freelancer's property. Contracts should only claim ownership 
of the specific deliverable, not the tools used to create it.

MORAL RIGHTS WAIVER
International contracts, especially those governed by US law, often include 
a waiver of moral rights. This means the client can modify your work, 
remove your name from it, or use it in ways you might find objectionable 
without your permission. Pakistani law does not have a strong moral rights 
framework but freelancers should be aware they are waiving any creative 
attribution claim.

PAYMENT TERMS
Standard international freelance payment terms are net-30, meaning the 
client pays within 30 days of invoice. Net-60 or net-90 is aggressive 
and common in large companies — it is worth flagging. Contracts should 
specify the currency of payment, the payment method, and who bears 
transfer fees. If the contract is silent on currency, this is a gap.

KILL FEE
A kill fee is compensation paid if the client cancels the project after 
work has begun. International contracts often have kill fee provisions. 
Pakistani freelancers should check if the contract specifies what they 
get paid if the project is cancelled halfway through. Absence of a kill 
fee clause is worth flagging as a missing clause.

REVISION POLICY
Contracts should specify how many rounds of revisions are included in 
the price and what happens if the client requests more. Contracts that 
give the client unlimited revision rights without additional payment 
are unfair and worth flagging.

CONFIDENTIALITY
It is standard for freelance contracts to include an NDA component 
requiring the freelancer not to disclose client information. This is 
not a red flag. However, NDAs that restrict the freelancer from 
mentioning the client relationship in a portfolio or CV are more 
restrictive and worth flagging as something to negotiate.

GOVERNING LAW AND DISPUTE RESOLUTION
International freelance contracts often specify that disputes are 
governed by the law of the client's country — US state law, UK law, 
EU law. This means if there is a dispute, the Pakistani freelancer 
would theoretically need to pursue it in a foreign court, which is 
impractical. Contracts should ideally specify international arbitration 
or Pakistani law for Pakistani freelancers. A foreign governing law 
clause is not necessarily a red flag but should be flagged as a 
watch out so the freelancer understands the practical implication.

INDEMNIFICATION
Many international contracts include an indemnification clause where 
the freelancer agrees to cover the client's legal costs if a third 
party sues the client because of the freelancer's work. Broad 
indemnification clauses with no cap on liability are aggressive red 
flags. The freelancer should push for indemnification to be limited 
to cases of the freelancer's proven negligence and capped at the 
total contract value.

LIABILITY CAP
Contracts should cap the freelancer's maximum liability at the total 
amount paid under the contract. Contracts with no liability cap expose 
the freelancer to unlimited financial risk and are a red flag.
