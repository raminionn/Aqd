NON-COMPETE CLAUSES IN PAKISTAN — STANDARDS AND LEGAL POSITION

LEGAL STATUS OF NON-COMPETES IN PAKISTAN
Non-compete clauses in Pakistan are governed by Section 27 of the Contract Act 
1872, which states that any agreement in restraint of trade is void. Pakistani 
courts have consistently held that overly broad non-compete agreements are 
unenforceable. However, courts do allow reasonable and limited restraints.

WHAT MAKES A NON-COMPETE REASONABLE IN PAKISTAN
A non-compete is more likely to be considered reasonable if it is limited to 
1 year or less in duration. Courts are unlikely to enforce non-competes longer 
than 1 year. The geographic scope should be limited — national or regional, 
not global. The scope of restricted activity should be specific — not "any 
competing business" but "direct competitors in the same product category." 
There should be some compensation or consideration for the restriction.

DURATION GUIDELINES
6 months non-compete: Standard and generally enforceable.
1 year non-compete: Acceptable and common in senior roles.
2 year non-compete: Aggressive, likely unenforceable under Pakistani law.
3 years or more: Almost certainly unenforceable and should be a red flag.

NON-SOLICITATION VS NON-COMPETE
Non-solicitation clauses (you cannot poach clients or employees) are treated 
differently from non-compete clauses and are more likely to be enforced. 
If a contract has both, analyse them separately. Non-solicitation for 1 to 2 
years is generally acceptable.

GARDEN LEAVE
Some Pakistani contracts include garden leave clauses where the employee is 
paid during the notice period but required to stay home and not work for 
competitors. This is a legitimate alternative to a non-compete during the 
notice period. If the employee is on garden leave and being paid, this is 
not a red flag.

INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY DURING EMPLOYMENT
All work created by an employee during employment in their job role belongs 
to the employer under Pakistani law. This includes code, designs, written 
materials, and inventions. This is standard and not a red flag. However, 
if the contract claims ownership of work done outside working hours on 
personal equipment, this is aggressive and worth flagging.

POST-EMPLOYMENT IP ASSIGNMENT
Contracts that require employees to assign IP created after termination are 
overreaching. If the contract says "any invention relating to our business 
for 2 years after termination belongs to us," this is a red flag.
